# Don't Take Generic Business Advice From VCs
**By Cédric Chin / Commoncog (October 2018)**
*Source: https://commoncog.com/dont-take-generic-business-advice-from-vcs/*

---

One of the things I was trying to do with my series on Chinese businessmen was to describe an approach to doing business that isn't rooted in startup culture.

Doing a startup is now a hot, aspirational, sexy goal for a sizeable percentage of my friends. It was far less sexy back when I was an 18 year old, reading Paul Graham essays on my bed when I should have been studying. I know people today who think that starting a company is their way out of the corporate rat race.

But here's the catch: having lived, breathed and optimised for startups for the better part of a decade, **I no longer think Silicon Valley culture makes sense for a large swath of people.** I'm not even sure startup culture makes sense in Asia. Instead, I currently believe that the startup approach isn't ideal for many industries, and that it doesn't work for most businesses.

---

## Optimising VC Outcomes

Pretty much everything I say here can be best understood through the lens of:

> "Startup wisdom is about optimising venture capital (VC) outcomes, so take everything you read about startups with the understanding that it's not about optimising outcomes for **you**."

Here's a concrete example: the **SME loop** — a ceiling you'll hit if you try to grow a small-to-medium enterprise to a large one. This ceiling is so universal that I posited the SME loop is a fundamental law of business.

**Conventional startup wisdom has nothing to say about the SME loop**, because growing a business to break out of it takes at least a decade of work. VCs can't benefit from a business that takes that long to grow large; the vast majority of VC funds are required to return their investments within a 10 year period.

It also means no VC will ever be able to invest in or benefit from the value created by billion dollar Asian conglomerates like Ng Teng Fong's Far East Organisation, or Robert Kuok's Wilmar International — business empires that took over four decades to build.

**The canon of startup advice literally pretends these companies do not exist.**

This matters to you as an individual entrepreneur, because if you only draw lessons from startup culture, you are prematurely and artificially limiting your paths to success.

---

## The Difference Is Time

Every venture-funded business is in a race against time to grow big and valuable. **Normal businesses are not.**

The false dichotomy that startup culture makes is to imply that the only way to bigness is through bone-crushing speed. This ignores the many Asian conglomerates that were built over two to three decades of consistent execution.

In truth, this need for speed exists because:
1. Startup VCs need to make a return within a certain time period
2. The companies that make the most returns for VCs are first to win in winner-takes-all markets

Startup investors know this, and so they encourage behaviours that seem counter-intuitive — like spending money excessively to gain an edge.

---

## Startup Wisdom Through the VC Lens

Everything in conventional startup wisdom may be understood through the lens of making a return for investors within a 10-year period:

| Startup Wisdom | Why VCs Want This |
|----------------|-------------------|
| Don't start without a cofounder | It's very stressful to grow a valuable company under 10 years alone |
| Pick a large, growing, overlooked market | Easier to grow valuable under 10 years |
| Play to VCs' fear of missing out | VCs need home runs to remain solvent |
| Iterate until product-market fit, then grow fast | Hockey-stick growth is how you grow valuable under 10 years |
| Move fast and break things | Growth above all |
| Don't worry about bad management at the start | Growth above all |
| Do whatever necessary for growth | Growth above all |
| Don't sell out early | VCs win when one company wins big |

The upshot: **startups are often a crapshoot.** The exhortation to grow at all costs leads to scorched earth practices. You either grow big to become a winner — or you die. The ones that don't win and don't die live an uncomfortable existence: their investors pressure them to get acqui-hired, or write them off as a bad deal.

## Removing the Deadline

Something interesting happens when you remove the artificial constraints of venture capital. Suddenly, the practices that Silicon Valley preaches fade into irrelevance.

When you're not under pressure to build a large, valuable company in a short amount of time, you have way more options:

- **Build a profitable business that supports your lifestyle** (Basecamp, Panic)
- **Build a profitable business that forms the basis of your empire** — then strategically take on investment over decades (Robert Kuok)
- **Build and sell a series of small, profitable businesses** — then parlay skills and capital into something bigger (Rob Walling)
- **Build a secondary income while employed** (Endcrawl)
- **Take on investment only after gaining experience** in a market (DS3)
- **Become a digital nomad** (Jon Yongfook)

The truth is that there are **an infinite number of ways to build a profitable, rewarding business**, and to adapt that business to your personal goals.

But because the set of paths are so varied, and because nobody stands to make a fortune from your taking them, **nobody writes about these paths.**

The path that gets the most attention is the path of the startup — and the constraints are so tight that Silicon Valley often says there's "only one way to do a startup."

---

## Key Takeaways for Your ELK Agent Startup

1. **Startup advice optimizes for VC outcomes, not yours** — A 10-year fund deadline fundamentally shapes what VCs recommend.

2. **Time is the difference** — VCs need speed. You might not. If you don't need 10x returns in 10 years, you have more options.

3. **The SME loop is real** — Breaking out of small-to-medium takes a decade or more. VC advice ignores this because they can't wait that long.

4. **Winner-takes-all is the VC bet** — They're betting you'll be the monopoly. That's a risky bet. If you're building a services business or niche tool, you might not need that play.

5. **More paths exist than startup culture acknowledges** — Building a profitable lifestyle business, building empire over decades, starting side income while employed — these are all valid, under-discussed paths.

6. **Ask: What outcome do I want?** — Not what VCs want. What do YOU want? Then reverse-engineer from there.
