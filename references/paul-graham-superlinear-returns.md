# Superlinear Returns
**By Paul Graham (May 2024)**
*Source: http://paulgraham.com/superlinear.html*

---

Most people are accustomed to linear returns. If you work twice as hard, you get twice as much done. If you work three times as hard, you get three times as much done.

But some of the most important things in life have superlinear returns. If you work twice as hard, you don't just get twice as much done — you might get 10x or 100x as much done, or you might find that you're working on an entirely different level.

## What Superlinear Returns Look Like

Imagine a graph with effort on the x-axis and results on the y-axis. With linear returns, this is a straight line. With superlinear returns, the line curves upward — the more effort you put in, the steeper the line gets.

Superlinear returns show up in many domains:

### In Business

The classic example is startups. If you start a company, the returns are not linear. If you do slightly better than average, you might not get slightly better returns — you might get zero returns, because the company fails. But if you do really well, you might get returns that are orders of magnitude better than average.

This is why startups are weird. The difference between doing really well and doing slightly well is not slightly better outcomes — it's between a successful company and a failed company. And the difference between doing really well and doing phenomenally well is again another order of magnitude.

### In Learning

If you learn twice as much, you don't just know twice as much. You know things that you couldn't have known otherwise. Knowledge compounds.

This is why some people seem to learn much faster than others. It's not that they're learning at a linearly faster rate. It's that they've reached a point where knowledge compounds, and they're on a steeper part of the curve.

### In Networks

The value of a network is proportional to the square of the number of users — Metcalfe's law. This is a classic example of superlinear returns.

But the same principle applies to social networks of people. If you know twice as many people, you don't get twice as many opportunities. You get many more, because you can connect people to each other.

## The Implications

Superlinear returns have some counterintuitive implications.

### Outliers Matter More Than Averages

In a world with linear returns, the average tells you a lot. If returns are linear and some people are doing well and some are doing poorly, the average is a good summary.

But in a world with superlinear returns, the average tells you almost nothing. The outlier at the top might have results that are orders of magnitude better than everyone else's combined.

This is why successful people often seem like outliers — because they are. In domains with superlinear returns, success doesn't look like being slightly above average. It looks like being in a completely different category.

### The Best Strategy Might Be Extreme

In a linear world, the best strategy is often moderation. You want to be somewhere in the middle — not too risky, not too safe.

But in a world with superlinear returns, the best strategy might be extreme. If you're going to work hard, really work hard. If you're going to start a company, really commit. Half measures might get you nothing.

### Small Differences Compound

In a linear world, small differences don't matter much. If you're 10% better, you get 10% more results.

But in a world with superlinear returns, small differences can compound into huge differences. That 10% advantage might compound into being 10x better over time.

This is why people who seem slightly ahead can pull away over time. They're not just slightly ahead — they're on a steeper part of the curve, and the distance is growing.

## Where Superlinear Returns Exist

Superlinear returns appear in domains where:

1. **There are economies of scale** — The more you have, the easier it is to get more
2. **There are winner-take-all effects** — The best gets much more than the second best
3. **There are network effects** — The value grows with the square of users
4. **There is compounding** — Knowledge, relationships, and reputation compound
5. **There are threshold effects** — You need to cross a threshold before results appear

Some domains with superlinear returns:
- Startups and investments
- Learning and expertise
- Reputation and audience building
- Networks and relationships
- Technology development

Some domains with more linear returns:
- Hourly wage work
- Most traditional jobs
- Activities without economies of scale

## How to Take Advantage

If you want to take advantage of superlinear returns:

### Find Domains With Superlinear Returns

The first step is to work in a domain that has superlinear returns. Startups have superlinear returns. Learning has superlinear returns. Building an audience has superlinear returns.

Hourly wage work does not. If you're in a domain with linear returns, consider whether you can move to a domain with superlinear returns.

### Focus on the Outlier Cases

In a domain with superlinear returns, the average case is not that interesting. The interesting cases are the outliers — the people and companies that are way up the curve.

Study these outliers. What did they do differently? What allowed them to reach the steep part of the curve?

### Be Willing to Go All In

In a domain with superlinear returns, half measures might get you nothing. There might be a threshold you need to cross before any results appear.

This is why successful founders often seem obsessed. They're not just working hard — they're trying to cross the threshold. And they know that if they don't cross it, they get nothing.

### Think in Exponents, Not Increments

In a linear world, you think in increments: "How can I be 10% better?"

In a world with superlinear returns, you think in exponents: "How can I be 10x better?" or "How can I reach a steeper part of the curve?"

The right question is not "How can I do slightly more?" The right question is "How can I get on a different curve?"

### Understand That Results Are Unfair

In domains with superlinear returns, results seem unfair. Two people can work similarly hard and get wildly different results, because one is on a steeper part of the curve.

This is why people who don't understand superlinear returns think success is mostly luck. They see people working similarly hard with wildly different results, and conclude it must be luck. But it might just be that one of them crossed a threshold while the other didn't.
