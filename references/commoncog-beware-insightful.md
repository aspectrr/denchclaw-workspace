# Beware What Sounds Insightful
**By Cédric Chin / Commoncog (September 2019)**
*Source: https://commoncog.com/beware-what-sounds-insightful/*

---

John D. Rockefeller Sr. was the richest tycoon of the Gilded Age. The rise of Rockefeller and his ilk also prompted the rise of the muckrakers — reform-minded journalists that exposed business leaders and politicians as corrupt.

Ida Tarbell was Rockefeller's muckraker nemesis. She is often credited with pioneering investigative journalism. But Tarbell stretched the truth about Rockefeller numerous times. As Ron Chernow writes in *Titan*:

> "Although Tarbell pretended to apply her scalpel to Standard Oil with surgical objectivity, she was never neutral... Her brother, William Walter Tarbell, had been a leading figure in forming the Pure Oil Company, the most serious domestic challenger to Standard Oil."

The tricks Tarbell used to stoke anger against Rockefeller still work today. But at the time, society had not developed antibodies against manipulative journalism.

## Being Critical in the Age of the Internet

If you read something on the internet today, the odds are good that the writing was produced to capture your attention. This isn't some profound realisation: many of us are familiar with the idea of an 'attention economy'. We're at the end of the second decade of the social Internet.

**I think the arms race for our attention has led to an arms race in writing.** The best online writers are able to make something sound insightful — regardless of whether it's true, or whether it's useful.

On one hand, this is the writer's job. Jason Zweig of the Wall Street Journal says his goal as a finance writer is to present a small handful of universal investing truths in increasingly novel ways, so readers never know he's repeating himself.

But at another level, online readers have to be increasingly critical when reading essays and blog posts for instrumental reasons.

This isn't some evil conspiracy. 'Writers optimising to produce insight porn to grab attention' sounds nefarious, but it's really just 'writers responding to the incentives of the social internet' — a simple side effect of the attention economy.

## Tricks That Make Things Sound Insightful

### Trick 1: Use a Story

I started this piece with a story. Preferably from a historical period that the reader isn't familiar with. This establishes that the writer knows something that the reader doesn't, and is communicating some argument with a basis in history.

Arguments from historical example are often doubly effective: first because they are rooted in reality, and second because they allow the writer to tell a story. Readers love stories — they're far easier to read than argumentative prose.

Sometimes, writers present obvious truths by way of a story. Morgan Housel is a master of this. In *Fat, Happy, And In Over Your Head*, he tells two stories: the first of WeWork and the second of Marcel Hirscher. The point? You can avoid the pain of loss aversion if you stop yourself from being greedy.

The idea is simple, but Housel's presentation is novel. Whether his assertion is practicable is another thing entirely.

### Trick 2: Repackage Obvious Truths

If a cliché is a truth that is commonly accepted, then an insight is a truth that isn't widespread. Clichés can thus be repackaged to sound insightful.

This is useful because:
- Clichés are often truths the reader already agrees with
- Whatever sounds insightful will keep the reader going

David Perell has a famous essay titled *Peter Thiel's Religion*. The essay is the length of a small booklet, divided into tiny sections, each ending with a packaged insight. A reader going through will keep scrolling because their brain goes "Oh, that's interesting! And that's interesting! And that's also interesting!"

**As a writer, I admire what he's done. But as a business person, nearly everything that Perell says in the piece about business is subtly wrong — enough to make me treat his essay as entertainment, not education.**

### Trick 3: Use Sophisticated Language

Sometimes simple ideas can be dressed up to seem more legitimate than they really are.

Economist Russ Roberts once said of Nassim Nicholas Taleb:

> "One view [of Fooled by Randomness] was: 'There is nothing original in this book. I didn't learn anything that I didn't know.' On the other hand, I learned something incredibly deep. That book grabbed me by the guts and jerked me around. It forced me to confront some things that I 'knew' but didn't really internalise."

Taleb's strategy of using stories and sophisticated language is designed to hammer home a simple set of ideas. Those ideas are difficult to grasp at the explicit level. So Taleb's approach is a legitimate use of this trick.

**Other times, however, if you strip away the complicated words, the underlying argument appears ridiculous.**

Venkatesh Rao is one of the best Internet writers I know. But his pieces are often optimised to sound insightful, as opposed to capturing some operationally useful truth about the world.

In *CEOs don't steer*, Rao writes:

> "The primary CEO function, and the trait the good ones are selected for, is to provide the gyroscopic stability required to keep a company vectored in the chosen direction. They end up in the jobs they do because they counterbalance an organization's natural tendency towards distraction, ADD and momentum dissipation."

This is a sophisticated sentence. But if you strip the essay of its sophisticated language, the core argument is: most CEOs are unintellectual dummies whose sole purpose is to keep the company on track, rowing in a single direction.

Rao's piece is ok if your goal is to read for entertainment. But it's not ok if your goal is to read for career reasons.

## The Takeaway

**What sounds insightful and what is useful or true are often not the same thing.**

This aspect of critical reading isn't emphasised as much as it should be. I think we are all the poorer for it.

A year ago I wrote *Writing Doesn't Make You a Genius*. I noticed that people tend to assume good writers are smarter than they actually are. I argued that this was mistaken — that writers sound smarter on paper because the act of writing forces them to clarify their ideas.

**But now I have another reason. Writers are often seen as smarter because good writers today are trained to optimise for sounding insightful.** This bleeds over into reader perception.

Whether a writer sounds smart or a piece sounds sophisticated shouldn't affect you if your goal is to put things you read to practice. The questions remain the same:

- "Is this person believable?"
- "How likely is this going to be useful?"
- "What's the cheapest way to find out?"

---

## Key Takeaways for Evaluating Startup Advice

1. **Stories ≠ Truth** — A good story makes something sound insightful, but doesn't make it true. Always ask: is this actionable?

2. **Repackaged Clichés** — If an essay feels like "insight! insight! insight!" keep scrolling, pause. The writer is repackaging obvious truths to keep you engaged.

3. **Sophisticated Language** — Strip away the complicated words. Is the underlying argument useful? Or does it fall apart?

4. **Believability Matters** — Who is this person? Have they done what they're advising? What's their track record?

5. **Cheapest Test** — What's the cheapest way to test if this advice is true for your situation? Don't just believe. Test.

6. **Entertainment vs. Education** — Some essays are entertainment dressed as education. That's fine, but know which you're consuming.
