# The Hardest Lessons for Startups to Learn
**By Paul Graham (April 2006)**
*Source: http://paulgraham.com/startuplessons.html*

---

The startups we've funded so far are pretty quick, but they seem quicker to learn some lessons than others. I think it's because some things about startups are kind of counterintuitive.

We've now invested in enough companies that I've learned a trick for determining which points are the counterintuitive ones: they're the ones I have to keep repeating.

## 1. Release Early

The thing I probably repeat most is this recipe for a startup: get a version 1 out fast, then improve it based on users' reactions.

By "release early" I don't mean you should release something full of bugs, but that you should release something minimal. Users hate bugs, but they don't seem to mind a minimal version 1, if there's more coming soon.

There are several reasons it pays to get version 1 done fast:

- This is simply the right way to write software, whether for a startup or not
- You don't know your users. No web startup does. Better to release something and let them tell you
- If you have no users, the initial release acts as a shakedown cruise — stress of getting that first version out will expose major problems early
- It makes you work harder. Problems in unreleased software are intriguing; problems in released software are alarming

## 2. Keep Pumping Out Features

Of course, "release early" has a second component: if you're going to start with something that doesn't do much, you better improve it fast.

What I find myself repeating is "pump out features." And this rule isn't just for the initial stages. This is something all startups should do for as long as they want to be considered startups.

By "feature" I mean one unit of hacking — one quantum of making users' lives better.

As with exercise, improvements beget improvements. If you run every day, you'll probably feel like running tomorrow. But if you skip running for a couple weeks, it will be an effort to drag yourself out. So it is with hacking: the more ideas you implement, the more ideas you'll have.

This is also a form of marketing. Users love a site that's constantly improving. In fact, users expect a site to improve. If you visit a site that seemed very good, and returned two months later and not one thing had changed, wouldn't it start to seem lame?

## 3. Make Users Happy

One thing all startups have in common is that they can't force anyone to do anything. They can't force anyone to use their software, and they can't force anyone to do deals with them. A startup has to sing for its supper.

When you're running a startup you feel like a little bit of debris blown about by powerful winds. The most powerful wind is users. They can either catch you and loft you up into the sky, as they did with Google, or leave you flat on the pavement, as they do with most startups.

The median visitor will arrive with their finger poised on the Back button. Your site has to say "Wait! Don't click on Back. This site isn't lame. Look at this, for example."

There are two things you have to do to make people pause:
1. Explain, as concisely as possible, what the hell your site is about
2. Give people everything you've got, right away — put your impressive stuff on the front page

## 4. Fear the Right Things

Another thing I find myself saying a lot is "don't worry." Actually, it's more often "don't worry about this; worry about that instead."

Startups are right to be paranoid, but they sometimes fear the wrong things.

Most visible disasters are not so alarming as they seem. Disasters are normal in a startup: a founder quits, you discover a patent that covers what you're doing, your servers keep crashing, you have to change your name, a deal falls through — these are all par for the course. They won't kill you unless you let them.

**What you should actually fear:**
- Not the established players (even Google). They're smart but not as motivated, and they have bureaucracy slowing them down
- Other startups you don't know exist yet — they're way more dangerous because, like you, they're cornered animals

Way more startups hose themselves than get crushed by competitors. The three main ways startups die:
1. Internal disputes
2. Inertia
3. Ignoring users

Almost everyone's initial plan is broken. If companies stuck to their initial plans, Microsoft would be selling programming languages, and Apple would be selling printed circuit boards. In both cases their customers told them what their business should be — and they were smart enough to listen.

## 5. Commitment Is a Self-Fulfilling Prophecy

The most important quality in a startup founder is **determination**. Not intelligence — determination.

This is a little depressing. I'd like to believe Viaweb succeeded because we were smart, not merely determined. But I've had an experience that convinced me otherwise: I spent several years living in New York.

You can lose quite a lot in the brains department and it won't kill you. But lose even a little bit in the commitment department, and that will kill you very rapidly.

Running a startup is like walking on your hands: it's possible, but it requires extraordinary effort. If an ordinary employee were asked to do the things a startup founder has to, he'd be very indignant.

If you lack commitment, chances are it will have been hurting you long before you actually quit. Everyone who deals with startups knows how important commitment is, so if they sense you're ambivalent, they won't give you much attention.

**The key insight:** If you make it clear you're going to succeed no matter what, and the only reason you need investors is to make it happen a little faster, you're much more likely to get money.

You can't fake this. The only way to convince everyone that you're ready to fight to the death is actually to be ready to.

## 6. There Is Always Room

I was talking recently to a startup founder about whether it might be good to add a social component to their software. He said he didn't think so, because the whole social thing was tapped out.

Really? So in a hundred years the only social networking sites will be Facebook, Instagram, and TikTok? Not likely.

There is always room for new stuff. At every point in history, people were discovering things that made everyone say "why didn't anyone think of that before?"

The limit on the number of startups is not the number that can get acquired — it's the amount of wealth that can be created. And I don't think there's any limit on that, except cosmological ones.

## 7. Don't Get Your Hopes Up

This was practically the corporate motto at Viaweb.

Startup founders are naturally optimistic. But you should treat your optimism the way you'd treat the core of a nuclear reactor: as a source of power that's also very dangerous. You have to build a shield around it, or it will fry you.

The shielding has to be pierced in a few places to let pipes in. I think the place to draw the line is between what you expect of yourself, and what you expect of other people. **It's ok to be optimistic about what you can do, but assume the worst about machines and other people.**

This is nowhere more important than with deals. If your startup is doing a deal, just assume it's not going to happen. The VCs who say they're going to invest in you aren't. The company that says they're going to buy you isn't. Then if things work out you can be pleasantly surprised.

## Speed, Not Money

The way I've described it, starting a startup sounds pretty stressful. It is. When I talk to founders, they all say: "I knew it would be hard, but I didn't realize it would be this hard."

So why do it? It would be worth enduring a lot of pain and stress to do something grand or heroic, but just to make money? Is making money really that important?

No, not really. I regard making money as a boring errand to be got out of the way as soon as possible.

So why do I spend so much time thinking about startups? **Economically, a startup is best seen not as a way to get rich, but as a way to work faster.** You have to make a living, and a startup is a way to get that done quickly, instead of letting it drag on through your whole life.

We take it for granted most of the time, but human life is fairly miraculous. It is also palpably short. By compressing the dull but necessary task of making a living into the smallest possible time, you show respect for life, and there is something grand about that.

## Key Takeaways for Your ELK Agent Startup

1. **Release early** — Don't wait for perfect. Get something in users' hands, then iterate.

2. **Pump out features** — Make your system better every day or two. Improvement begets improvement.

3. **Make users happy** — Explain what you do in 1-2 sentences. Put your best stuff on the front page.

4. **Fear the right things** — Not Google or competitors. Fear ignoring users, inertia, and internal disputes.

5. **Determination > Intelligence** — You need both, but determination is the non-negotiable one.

6. **There's always room** — The ELK space isn't "tapped out." There's room for better tools.

7. **Don't get your hopes up** — With deals, assume they won't happen. Keep building.

8. **Startups are about speed, not money** — Compress the work, respect life, build fast.
