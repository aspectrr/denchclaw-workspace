### Naval Ravikant - Wealth Creation
**File:** `naval-wealth-creation.md`

Key themes:
- **Specific knowledge:** Cannot be taught. Found through genuine curiosity. Often looks like obsession.
- **Leverage types:** Labor, capital (permissioned) vs. code, media (permissionless)
- **Wealth formula:** Specific knowledge + Leverage + Accountability + Judgment
- **The insight:** You won't get rich renting out your time. You need equity and products that scale.

Key quote:
> "Productize yourself. You are not going to get rich renting out your time. You must own equity — a piece of a business — to gain your financial freedom."

## How to Use These References

### When You're Just Starting (Validation Phase)
1. Read: **Do Things That Don't Scale** — The principle of manual first, then productize
2. Apply: Talk to ELK engineers manually, debug their pipelines by hand, learn what hurts

### When You're Building (Product Phase)
1. Read: **The Hardest Lessons** — Release early, keep pumping out features
2. Read: **Founder Mode** — Stay involved in details, don't just delegate
3. Apply: Ship minimal v1, iterate based on real user pain

### When You're Scaling (Growth Phase)
1. Read: **Frighteningly Ambitious Ideas** — Think bigger, start small
2. Read: **The Age of Abundance** — Position for AI agents transforming work
3. Apply: Your initial product might be too small — what's the bigger vision?

## Highest Order Bit Thinking

### What "Highest Order Bit" Means

In computing, the "highest order bit" (most significant bit) determines whether a number is positive or negative — it has the biggest impact on the value.

In startups, the **highest order bit** is the decision or insight that creates the most leverage. Everything else is lower-order.

**Paul Graham's highest order bits:**
1. Make something users want (the most fundamental)
2. Get a version 1 out fast
3. Determination > intelligence
4. Start with what works, expand toward the ambitious

**For your ELK agent startup, the highest order bit:**
- **Problem:** ELK engineers spend 70-80% of time debugging pipeline issues
- **Insight:** An AI agent that can read cluster state, logs, and suggest fixes
- **Leverage:** Augment engineers, don't replace them — they'll adopt tools that make them more effective

## Quick Reference: The Minimalist Entrepreneur Pattern

If you're following the minimalist entrepreneur approach (validation before building):

1. **Validate first** — Manual conversations, not building
2. **Learn patterns** — What do ELK engineers struggle with?
3. **Sell the manual service** — $500 debugging, $2k/month managed
4. **Then productize** — Build what you learned manually

The reference you already have (`paul-graham-do-things-that-dont-scale.md`) is the foundation for this approach.
