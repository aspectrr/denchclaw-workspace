# How to Do Great Work
**By Paul Graham (July 2021)**
*Source: http://paulgraham.com/greatwork.html*

[This is a condensed version of Paul Graham's comprehensive essay on doing great work. Full essay available at the link above.]

---

If you want to do great work, it helps to understand what great work is. The best way to understand it is to look at examples of great work.

Great work has two parts: doing something, and doing it well. You can't do great work without doing something, and you can't do it well without understanding what "well" means for that thing.

## Finding Great Work

When you're trying to find what to work on, you're really trying to find your personal optimum of three overlapping circles:

1. **What you're good at**
2. **What you enjoy**
3. **What makes an impact**

The ideal work for you is at the intersection of all three. If you're lucky, you'll find work that's naturally at the intersection. If not, you may have to push one of the circles toward overlap.

### What You're Good At

You have natural abilities. These are things you do better than most people without trying. They're your comparative advantage.

But here's the trap: most people's natural abilities are also their natural interests. Usually, the things you're good at are the things you enjoy. This makes the problem easier, but also makes it harder to recognize — because when you're good at something, it feels easy, and you discount it.

**The mistake people make**: they look at what they're good at and think "I can't make a career out of this, it's too easy." But ease is exactly the signal you're looking for.

### What You Enjoy

Work you don't enjoy is rarely great. The energy required to do great work is enormous, and you can't sustain that energy if you don't enjoy it.

But people are often confused about what they enjoy. They think they enjoy the praise they get for doing something, when what they actually enjoy is the work itself. Or they think they enjoy something because they're good at it, when they actually just enjoy being good at things.

Here's a test: if you couldn't tell anyone what you were doing, would you still want to do it?

### What Makes an Impact

Great work is work that makes a difference. It changes something. It creates something new. It opens up possibilities.

The highest-impact work tends to have these characteristics:
- **It's fundamental** — It affects the layer underneath, not just the layer on top
- **It's broadly applicable** — It affects many people or problems
- **It's durable** — It still matters 10 years from now

## The Shape of Great Work

Great work often looks different from ordinary work. It's not just better — it's qualitatively different.

### Independent

Great work tends to be more independent of existing work. It's not just an incremental improvement on what came before. It opens up a new direction.

The people who do great work often seem to be doing something obviously wrong — until it turns out they were right.

### Original

Great work is more original than ordinary work. But this is a tricky quality because originality is mostly detected in retrospect. When something is truly original, it often looks wrong or stupid or both.

The test: if your idea doesn't seem obviously wrong to most people, maybe it's not original enough.

### Hard

Great work is usually harder than ordinary work. But it's not just harder in degree — it's harder in kind. It's the kind of hard where you have to figure out what to do, not just do it.

The people who do great work tend to seek out problems that are harder than they know how to solve. They find this energizing rather than daunting.

## Learning What's Possible

One of the most important things you can do is learn what's possible. Most people have a very limited idea of what's possible, and this limits them more than any lack of ability.

When you see someone do something, you realize it's possible. Until then, you literally couldn't imagine it. This is why being around people who do great work is so valuable.

If you're trying to do great work, you should:
- Read the best work in your field
- Look at what the best people are doing
- Try to understand what makes it different from ordinary work

## The Process

Great work doesn't happen in a flash of insight. It comes from a process:

1. **Notice something** — A pattern, a gap, a question, an anomaly
2. **Get curious** — Why is this interesting? What does it mean?
3. **Dig deeper** — Follow the thread wherever it leads
4. **Try things** — Build prototypes, test hypotheses, make experiments
5. **Iterate** — Refine what works, abandon what doesn't
6. **Ship** — Get it out into the world

The people who do great work tend to be people who follow this process repeatedly. They're constantly noticing things, getting curious, and following threads.

### The Importance of Projects

Great work usually comes from working on a project. A project is a container for your curiosity. It gives you a reason to learn things, try things, and iterate.

Without a project, curiosity is just daydreaming. With a project, curiosity becomes work.

Good projects have these properties:
- They're specific enough to be tractable
- They're general enough to be interesting
- They're ambitious enough to be worth doing
- They're achievable enough to be worth starting

### The Importance of Peers

Great work tends to cluster. The people who do great work tend to know each other, work with each other, and inspire each other.

If you want to do great work, get to know the people doing great work in your field. Not just their work, but them. Go to conferences. Send emails. Ask questions. Be interested.

The best peers will:
- Challenge your ideas
- Teach you things you didn't know
- Connect you to other people
- Model what great work looks like

## What Great Work Looks Like

How do you know if you're doing great work? Here are some signs:

### You're Learning

Great work involves continuous learning. If you're not learning, you're probably not doing great work.

### It's Hard But Not Frustrating

Great work is hard, but it's not frustrating. When you're doing great work, the difficulty feels like an interesting problem, not an obstacle.

### Time Disappears

When you're doing great work, you lose track of time. You're not watching the clock. You're absorbed in the work.

### People Are Surprised

Great work surprises people. Not necessarily in a flashy way, but in a "I didn't expect that to work" way.

### It Opens New Possibilities

Great work doesn't just solve a problem — it opens up new problems or new approaches. It creates more work, not less.

---

## Key Takeaways for Your AI Agent Startup

1. **Find the intersection** — You need to be good at it, enjoy it, AND it needs to matter. All three circles.

2. **Ease is a signal** — The things you're naturally good at feel easy. That's not a bug, it's a feature.

3. **Learn what's possible** — Your biggest limitation is often not ability, but imagination. Be around people doing great work.

4. **Projects give curiosity direction** — Don't just be curious. Be curious about something specific that you're trying to build.

5. **Seek hardness of the right kind** — Great work is hard because you're figuring out what to do, not because you're grinding through known problems.

6. **Time disappears when it's right** — If you're watching the clock, you might be in the wrong work.
