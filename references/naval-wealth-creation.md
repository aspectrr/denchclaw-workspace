## The Foundations

Naval Ravikant is an entrepreneur, investor, and philosopher. His thoughts on wealth creation have become essential reading for founders. Here are the key principles.

### Making Wealth Is a Skill, Not Luck

> "Making money is not a thing you do; it's a skill you learn."

Many people believe wealth is a matter of luck — being in the right place at the right time. Naval argues that wealth creation is a learnable skill that compounds over time. The people who "got lucky" often made their own luck through specific knowledge, good judgment, and understanding of leverage.

### Productize Yourself

> "Productize yourself. You are not going to get rich renting out your time. You must own equity — a piece of a business — to gain your financial freedom."

You cannot get wealthy by selling your time at an hourly rate. Time is finite and linear. To build wealth, you need:
- **Equity** — Ownership in a business
- **Product** — Something that scales without your direct time
- **Yourself** — Your unique knowledge, skills, and position

Combine all three: **Productize what's uniquely you.**

## Specific Knowledge

### What It Is

> "Specific knowledge is found by pursuing your genuine curiosity rather than what's 'hot' right now."

Specific knowledge is knowledge that cannot be easily taught or trained. It's knowledge that:
- You learned through apprenticeship or self-teaching
- Cannot be written down in a book or course
- Often looks like a quirk or obsession
- Is usually discovered by pursuing genuine curiosity

**Examples:**
- A salesperson's intuition about when to close
- A programmer's feel for clean architecture
- A founder's sense for product-market fit
- An engineer's deep understanding of a specific system

### Why It Matters

Specific knowledge is your competitive advantage. It can't be outsourced or automated because it's specific to you. It can't be taught in schools because it's too specific and context-dependent.

**For your ELK agent startup:** Your specific knowledge is that you've lived the pain of maintaining ELK stacks. You understand the hair-on-fire problems that generic engineers don't see. That's your specific knowledge.

### How to Build It

1. **Follow genuine curiosity** — Not what's "hot," but what genuinely interests you
2. **Apprentice with masters** — Learn from the best in your field
3. **Build in public** — Create a body of work that demonstrates your knowledge
4. **Embrace the weird** — Specific knowledge often looks like an obsession to others

## Leverage

### Three Types of Leverage

Leverage is what multiplies your output. There are three types:

| Type | Permission Needed? | Source | Scale |
|------|---------------------|--------|-------|
| **Labor** | Yes (need to hire) | People working for you | Linear |
| **Capital** | Yes (need money) | Money working for you | Superlinear |
| **Code** | No | Software working for you | Exponential |
| **Media** | No | Content working for you | Exponential |

### Labor and Capital vs. Code and Media

**Labor and Capital require permission.** You need people to agree to work for you. You need investors to give you money.

**Code and Media do not require permission.** You can write software on your laptop. You can create content and publish it. No one can stop you.

This is why code and media are the highest leverage activities. They're permissionless leverage.

**Code:** Write a piece of software once, it can serve millions of users. Marginal cost of replication is near zero.

**Media:** Create content once, it can reach millions of people. Podcasts, blogs, videos — all scale without marginal cost.

### The Best Founders Use All Four

The best founders combine all four types of leverage:
- **Labor:** A small, excellent team
- **Capital:** Funding or revenue to invest
- **Code:** Software that scales
- **Media:** Content that spreads their message

## Accountability

### Why It Matters

> "Take accountability for your work. If you fail, you want the failure attributed to you. Not because you like failing, but because you want to be able to fix it."

Accountability gives you:
- **Learning** — When things fail and it's attributed to you, you can learn and fix
- **Trust** — People trust those who take accountability
- **Asymmetric risk** — If you succeed, you get outsized credit; if you fail, you can try again

### The Accountability Leak

If you're succeeding but not getting credit, you have an accountability leak. Someone is taking credit for your work. Fix the leak.

If you're failing but not being blamed, you have an accountability insulation. You're not learning from failure. Remove the insulation.

## Judgment

### The CEO Skill

Judgment is the ability to make good decisions with incomplete information. This is the primary skill of CEOs and investors.

Judgment comes from:
- **Experience** — Making many decisions and seeing the outcomes
- **Pattern matching** — Recognizing situations from past experience
- **Mental models** — Frameworks for thinking about problems

### Judgment vs. Knowledge

Knowledge is knowing facts. Judgment is knowing what to do with those facts.

You can have all the knowledge in the world and still have poor judgment. Judgment is developed through making decisions, seeing outcomes, and learning from mistakes.

**For your ELK agent startup:** You have knowledge of ELK. But judgment is knowing which problems are worth solving, which users are worth targeting, and which features are worth building.

## The Wealth Creation Formula

### To Create Wealth

> "Wealth is assets that earn while you sleep."

Combine:
1. **Specific knowledge** — What only you know
2. **Leverage** — Code, media, capital, labor
3. **Accountability** — Taking ownership of outcomes
4. **Judgment** — Making good decisions

### The Pattern

1. **Learn** — Develop specific knowledge in a domain
2. **Build** — Create a product that leverages that knowledge
3. **Own** — Take equity, not just salary
4. **Scale** — Use code and media for permissionless leverage

## Wealth vs. Money vs. Status

### Definitions

- **Wealth** is assets that earn while you sleep. Businesses, code, media, investments.
- **Money** is how we transfer wealth. Money is the medium of exchange.
- **Status** is your position in the social hierarchy. Status is a zero-sum game.

### The Trap

Many people pursue status instead of wealth. Status is zero-sum — for you to move up, someone else has to move down. This creates conflict.

Wealth is positive-sum — you can create wealth without taking from others. When you build a useful product, you've created wealth. Both you and your customers are better off.

**For your ELK agent startup:** You're creating wealth by building something that helps engineers. You're not taking from anyone. This is positive-sum.
